Understanding Laryngeal Contributions in Adult OSA

Expanded Findings and Clinical Implications

Abstract

Background: Traditional OSA models focus on oropharyngeal collapse, but emerging evidence supports the involvement of laryngeal structures, particularly the epiglottis.

Objective: Evaluate inter-rater reliability of laryngeal features observed during DISE and explore clinical associations.

Key Results: Epiglottic prolapse was more common in younger patients and showed moderate inter-rater reliability, while features like post-cricoid redundancy showed only fair agreement. Partial laryngectomy patients had altered laryngeal architecture and worse OSA severity.

Why the Larynx Matters

Epiglottic prolapse is increasingly recognized as a contributor to OSA in younger patients and those with CPAP intolerance.

Our study found that epiglottic collapse is not only identifiable with moderate reliability but may be present even in patients with mild or unresponsive OSA. This suggests that targeted treatments like epiglottopexy could benefit select individuals who do not respond to palatal or hypopharyngeal surgery.

Study Highlights

  • Moderate agreement for epiglottic prolapse across reviewers
  • Slight-to-fair reliability for laryngomalacia and post-cricoid redundancy
  • Younger patients disproportionately affected by epiglottic collapse
  • Partial laryngectomy linked to epiglottic deformation and increased OSA severity

Clinical Relevance

Findings underscore the need for:

  • Standardized DISE protocols including laryngeal assessment
  • Advanced imaging (3D/CFD) to better visualize airflow obstruction
  • Training modules for consistent recognition of subtle laryngeal anomalies

Future Directions

Prospective, multicenter studies are needed to:

  • Clarify the prevalence of laryngeal-related OSA phenotypes
  • Integrate AI to assist in rating and classification during DISE
  • Investigate sensory deficits and neuromuscular mechanisms of collapse

Conclusion

Laryngeal structures, particularly the epiglottis, are emerging as key contributors in select OSA populations. Improved diagnostic accuracy and personalized interventions could transform management strategies for these patients.
