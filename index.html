Comprehensive Abstract

Study Overview

Laryngeal morphological features contribute significantly to airway obstruction in obstructive sleep apnea (OSA), though standardized evaluation methods remain limited. We conducted a retrospective analysis of fiberoptic laryngoscopy videos from 142 patients (88 OSA, 54 controls) to investigate specific laryngeal anomalies and quantify reliability across multiple features including laryngomalacia, arytenoid position, epiglottic shape/prolapse, and post-cricoid redundancy.

Expanded Discussion

Clinical Significance of Laryngeal Involvement

The findings emphasize the critical yet often overlooked role of laryngeal structures, particularly the epiglottis, in OSA pathophysiology. While conventional models focus on oropharyngeal collapse, our data demonstrate that laryngeal features represent an independent anatomical consideration.

Epiglottic Phenotype

Younger patients show more epiglottic involvement, potentially indicating unique etiological mechanisms independent of age-related tissue laxity. This may explain treatment failures in select patients unresponsive to conventional therapy.

Assessment Challenges

The marked inter-reviewer disparities (8.3% vs 23.4% for epiglottic prolapse) highlight fundamental challenges in standardized assessment. This variability likely reflects differences in examiner thresholds rather than true measurement error.

CPAP Resistance

Epiglottic collapse may worsen with positive pressure by pushing the epiglottis into the laryngeal aditus. This mechanism explains why some patients experience treatment failures with CPAP despite adequate pressure titration.

Surgical Interventions

Site-specific surgeries (epiglottopexy, stiffening, partial epiglottectomy) show 75-90% success rates. Systematic reviews report safe and effective outcomes, with significant AHI reductions in properly selected patients.

Need for Standardization

Priority Areas for Improvement

  • Operationalized Definitions: Clear, measurable criteria for what constitutes "abnormal" findings
  • Training Protocols: Structured programs requiring >100 evaluations to achieve expert-level proficiency
  • Advanced Imaging: 3D reconstruction, dynamic cine MRI, or computational fluid dynamics for objective assessment
  • AI-Assisted Scoring: Deep learning models achieving 70% F1 scores for automated DISE assessment
  • Dynamic Evaluation: DISE identifies 10-40% epiglottic collapse vs 11% by awake exam alone

Future Research Directions

  • Genetic and collagen-related drivers of laryngeal laxity in younger patients
  • Laryngeal sensory deficits (38-61% show abnormal sensation) and correlation with morphology
  • Laryngopharyngeal reflux (49% prevalence) creating inflammation-collapse cycle
  • Superior laryngeal nerve stimulation as novel neuromodulation approach
  • Prospective outcomes studies correlating morphology with treatment response
  • Multi-level surgical approaches combining epiglottic surgery with hypoglossal nerve stimulation
